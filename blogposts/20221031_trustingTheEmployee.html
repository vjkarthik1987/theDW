Post 4 - Trusting the employee

31 Oct 2022

In the Austrian capital city of Vienna sits a small restaurant called Der Wiener Deewan, which was established in 2005 and is still going strong.

Yeah, so what - you may ask. Why did we tell this now? We told you this because it is a successful social experiment where there is no set price for the items on the menu. Customers are allowed to pay what they wish and eat as they like - no compulsions. As the founder, a Pakistani immigrant Afzal Deewan, says, ‘They just decided to trust the customer.’ And the fact that they are still going strong after 17 years shows that the idea has worked.

And where do we stand when it comes to employees and organizations? Pretty bad actually. We know organizations where a $2 expense will have to go through 2 levels of approval and will take 2 months to get reimbursed. We also know organizations where people are asked to write down their name.

And at the other end of the spectrum are companies like Netflix and Basecamp. The radical Basecamp has a ‘no-limits’ expense policy. It provides each employee with an American Express credit card in their name to spend on travel, office supplies, and software. Netflix has a travel and expense policy that in summary states - anything is acceptable as long as it is in the best interest of Netflix. U2i, the Polish company, trusts their employees with a company wallet and expects them to do only one thing - spend the money as if it were their own.

A lot of things that companies do today are because they do not trust their employees - tracking time, installing surveillance software, asking for daily updates, and asking employees to switch on the web camera all the time. Even the push towards a back-to-office movement is also because managers and executive management do not trust people to deliver results by themselves. A sad consequence of management by walking around.

It is time that organizations start trusting employees a bit more, or in fact, a whole lot more. And then they will get the trust back.
